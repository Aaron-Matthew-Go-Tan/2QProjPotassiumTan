What is a Mass Extinction?




Extinction, the complete disappearance of a species, is a normal occurrence — it helps clear paths for new species to take center stage. However, what isn’t normal is when extinction rates increase drastically. Burges (2021) defines a mass extinction as “a time where the rate of species extinction is greater than the rate of species creation”. In fact, the most notable cases involved the loss of over 75% of all species when they happened!

These events are caused by disasters that disrupt ecosystems all around the world. For instance, the first mass extinction, the Ordovician extinction, was caused by the lowering of sea levels due to global cooling. This reduced the marine habitats organisms at the time like the "sea scorpion" Jaekelopterus (shown on the left) lived in.

'
Other examples include the Late Devonian and Permian extinctions, which saw an imbalance of atmospheric gases and seismic and volcanic activity. However, the most famous of the mass extinctions is the Cretaceous extinction, which featured an asteroid strike (shown on the right) setting a chain of events that wiped out the dinosaurs, among many other species.

It may not seem like it, but humans are the cause of yet another mass extinction: the Holocene extinction. Ever since the last ice age 10,000 years ago, humans have been causing a multitude of species to go extinct. In addition, pollution, poaching, and deforestation only make the situation even more bleak...
